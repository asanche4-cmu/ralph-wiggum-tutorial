Something I learned related to this assignment was how important it is to have clear requirements and good task splitting to give our agentic workers. Having clear requirements lets us give a clear definition of done to our agentic pipelines, which reduces the number of wasted iterations each loop takes. I also ran into a tension I was not expecting, that the clearer my requirements got the more context each iteration had to read, so better requirements lowered the number of loops while raising the cost of each one. Even with my clear requirements and my Claude generated prompts, the amount of tokens I spent was ~$31! This opened my eyes to how important balancing token usage versus value produced will be in our industry. Agentic pipeline isn't just about correctness, it's about the cost-per-value of every loop. Finding mechanisms to run these loops more efficiently will become an integral part of anyone doing loop engineering in this world where token prices are dictated by companies out of our control. I also learned from this experience that sometimes you are going to get better value out of the agentic loop depending on how you plan out your stages. In my iteration of the exercise I decided to have a "build reviewer" to review the work that was produced by the pipeline. On my first iteration, the reviewer output wasn't very useful and it wasn't critical of the work done. On my second iteration, it did output some useful feedback but still was pretty lenient on the work done by the build implementer. I wonder if using another model would have produced different and more critical reviews making the pipeline more solid. All in all, planning out the stages in the pipeline is also important and doing several trial and error runs on a script will increase the quality of the pipeline over time.
